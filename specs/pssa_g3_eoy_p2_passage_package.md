## 2. Passage

Mateo was not supposed to play ball in the house. His grandmother told him so every week, in the same gentle voice. "Feet are for running outside," she would say. "Inside, we walk." But this Saturday it was raining hard, the living room was wide and empty, and the foam ball felt too harmless to cause any trouble.

He bounced it once against the wall. The sound was satisfying, so he bounced it again. On the third throw, his hand slipped, and the ball sailed higher than he meant it to. Mateo's eyes followed it as it curved toward the tall shelf by the window — the shelf where his grandmother kept the blue vase.

That vase was not an ordinary one. It was painted with small white birds, and his grandmother had carried it carefully from the house where she grew up, far across the ocean, when she was younger than Mateo was now. She dusted it every Sunday. She called it the one thing she could never replace.

The ball struck the vase with a soft thud. For half a second, nothing happened. Then the vase tipped, rolled off the shelf, and dropped to the wooden floor. It broke into three large pieces, and many tiny shards scattered across the boards like blue snow.

Mateo froze. His heart pounded, and his stomach tied in a knot. He looked toward the doorway, sure his grandmother would come running, but the kitchen was far away and the rain was loud, and no one came. The house stayed quiet. Only Mateo and the broken vase knew what had happened.

A thought slipped into his head, quick and sneaky. He could clean it up himself. He could hide the pieces at the bottom of the trash, under the empty cereal box, and his grandmother might think the cat had knocked the vase down. The cat was always climbing where it should not. No one would ever know it was him.

He reached toward the largest piece — then stopped. Its edges were sharp, and his hand had begun to shake. Instead of picking it up, he pictured his grandmother on Sunday, reaching to dust the shelf and finding the empty spot bare. He pictured her searching the house for the vase, and wondering what happened, and never knowing the truth. Hiding the pieces would not bring the vase back. It would only put a secret between them — and a secret, he thought, was the one thing he could never sweep up. The knot in his stomach pulled tighter.

Mateo sat back on his heels and let out a slow breath. Then he stood and walked toward the kitchen. Each step felt heavy, as if his shoes were filled with sand. He found his grandmother by the window, quietly folding a stack of warm towels. She looked up and smiled, and for a moment Mateo almost lost his nerve.

"Abuela," he said, and his voice came out small. "I broke the blue vase. I was bouncing my ball inside, even though you told me not to. It hit the shelf, and the vase fell."

His grandmother set the towels down. She was quiet for a long moment, and Mateo watched her face change — first surprise, then a deep, tired sadness. He looked down at his shoes, sure he had spoiled the whole rainy day.

But when she spoke, her voice was not angry. "Thank you for coming to find me, Mateo," she said softly. She knelt down so they were eye to eye. "I am very sad about the vase. It carried a little piece of my old home inside it." She was quiet again. "But you did not let me wonder, and you did not blame the cat. I will remember that."

Mateo felt the knot in his stomach loosen, just a little. It did not disappear — he was still sorry, and the vase was still in pieces — but the heavy, sandy feeling began to lift from his shoes.

"Now," Abuela said, rising to her feet, "the ball goes away for the rest of the day. Please put it in your room." Mateo nodded and did exactly that, setting the foam ball deep in his closet.

When he came back, his grandmother had brought a broom and a dustpan. "These pieces are sharp," she said. "We clean them the safe way, and we do it together." She swept the tiny shards into the dustpan while Mateo held it steady, both of them careful to keep their fingers away from the jagged edges. The three big pieces she did not throw out. She wrapped them gently in an old cloth.

"What should we do with these?" she asked, and she truly seemed to be asking him. Mateo thought hard. "Maybe we could glue them," he said. "Or… you always say a broken thing can become something new." For the first time since the kitchen, his grandmother smiled. "We could press the pieces into a stepping stone for the garden," she said. "A place to keep the little birds." Together they set the wrapped bundle on the shelf, in the empty spot, until they could decide for sure.

That night, Mateo lay in bed and remembered the long, sandy walk to the kitchen, and how much lighter his shoes had felt on the way back. The vase was still broken, and he was still sorry. But the house did not feel heavy anymore. And he decided that the next time the rain kept him inside, his feet would remember to walk.

## 4. Section / structure map (drives A-K.1.1.3 plot grid)

Single continuous narrative, no headings (literary). Character arc, in order:
1. **Setup / rule established** — grandmother's "inside we walk"; rainy Saturday; the foam ball seems harmless.
2. **Inciting incident** — third bounce slips; ball hits the **blue vase** (the irreplaceable heirloom); it falls and breaks into three large pieces and many shards.
3. **Temptation / private struggle** — no one comes; Mateo considers hiding the pieces and blaming the cat; reaches toward a piece but **stops** (sharp edges; shaking hand); privately pictures the empty shelf and the secret between them ("stomach tied in a knot").
4. **Choice / confession** — heavy walk to the kitchen ("shoes filled with sand"); he almost loses his nerve; tells Abuela plainly what he did and that he disobeyed.
5. **Response** — she is sad but thanks him for coming to find her and not blaming the cat; the knot loosens.
6. **Restorative resolution (consequence + repair)** — the ball is put away for the day; they clean up the sharp pieces **safely, together** (Abuela brings broom + dustpan); they save the large pieces and decide together to make them into a garden stepping stone; Mateo resolves to walk inside next time.

The **message is shown, not stated** — the reader infers "owning a mistake / honesty matters even when it is hard" from the arc, not from a narrator maxim.
