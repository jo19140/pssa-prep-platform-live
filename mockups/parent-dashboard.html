What you can do at home (if you want)

None of this is required. The program is doing the teaching. These help a little, if you have time.

  • 📚
    Read any book together for 10 minutes. Even one she chooses for fun. Stop on 2–3 words she doesn't know.
  • 🚗
    Use her new words in conversation. "I'm reluctant to go to the store in this rain" lands harder than any flashcard.
  • 🙌
    Notice when she figures something out. "You worked through that hard part" beats "you're so smart" by a mile.

Design notes for review · parent autopilot view

  • No teacher anywhere. This is the parent-pay product — there is no interventionist in the loop. The "program" is the subject of every sentence, including the warm sign-off card at the bottom.
  • Hero is a sentence, not a number. "Maya gained another month of reading this week" is the kind of thing a parent screenshots and texts to grandma. The metrics are supporting evidence, not the headline.
  • "What you can do at home (if you want)." The "if you want" matters. The product promises the parent doesn't have to do anything. Anything they do is bonus. This protects parents who already feel guilty about not being able to help with reading.
  • "Talk to a real teacher" button. Upsell hook to a paid human tier later — or just an off-ramp to support. Either way, low-pressure presence keeps the human option visible without making it required.
  • Bilingual is a chip, not a feature. "ver en español" at the bottom of the warmest section signals that Spanish-speaking households are welcome here, without making English-speaking users navigate around a translation tool.
  • "A note from the program." Soft anthropomorphization. Not creepy, not cute — just enough warmth that the parent feels accompanied. This is where the product earns the right to be "the teacher."
  • No streaks gamification on the parent surface. The streak shows up in "what she did this week" but it's framed as the kid's accomplishment, not a metric the parent is supposed to chase. Parents should feel relief on this page, not anxiety.
