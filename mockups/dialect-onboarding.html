Dialect & home-language onboarding · the sensitive UX

Powered by the cross-language sound-spelling transfer charts and AAE linguistic differences guide in your uploaded references. The product purpose: when a kid pronounces "tooth" as "toof" (AAE) or "sheep" as "cheep" (Spanish-influenced phonology), the diagnostic should not count that as a decoding error. Most reading platforms either ignore dialect entirely or try to "correct" it. We do neither — we listen the right way.

Every step below is skip-able. Default state if the family skips everything: the program assumes mainstream-American English phonology and notes when a session has many systematic pronunciations that suggest a dialect, prompting the family to come back here.

Step 1 of 4 · why we're asking
👂

Help us listen the right way

When the program listens to your child read, the way certain sounds get pronounced depends on the languages and dialects spoken at home. We want to know about those so we don't accidentally mark something as a "mistake" when it's actually a perfectly correct sound from your family's way of speaking.

You can skip any of this. If you do, we'll just listen with standard American English ears, and you can come back anytime.

Example

If your child says "toof" for tooth, that's a perfectly normal sound in African American English. We'd notice the systematic pattern and not count it against their decoding.

If your child says "cheep" for sheep, that's expected when Spanish is the home language. Same idea.

Step 2 of 4 · languages spoken at home

Which languages do you speak at home?

Pick all that apply. There's no wrong answer.

What does "Sound transfer ready" mean?

For these languages, we have a published reference chart that tells us which English sounds typically transfer from the home language and which need extra practice. We use that chart to listen more accurately during your child's reading sessions. Languages not listed will fall back to default English ears — you can still pick them, we just won't have the same custom listening.

Step 3 of 4 · how your family talks

Anything else we should know about how you talk?

This is optional. We ask because regional and community ways of speaking are real and beautiful — and we want to recognize them, not flatten them.

What we're not doing
  • We're not asking about your race or ethnicity. This is only about how you talk at home.
  • We're not going to teach your child their way of talking is wrong. It isn't.
  • We will teach them how mainstream-school English works, because that's what tests are written in — but as an addition, not a replacement.

Design notes · the high-risk UX

  • Why this matters commercially. Most reading platforms silently mismark dialect-using kids and quietly inflate their "decoding gap." A platform that does this right is rare in this market. It is a major B2B-to-urban-districts differentiator AND an emotional differentiator with parents of color who have watched their kids be misdiagnosed for years.
  • Why this is dangerous if done wrong. The wrong copy here looks like the product is asking about race or treating non-mainstream English as a deficiency to correct. The Step 3 amber box ("what we're NOT doing") is non-negotiable — it answers the worry before it forms.
  • Skip is a first-class option at every step. Buttons: "Skip all of this," "Maybe later," "Skip this step." A family that doesn't want to answer never has to. The product still works.
  • "Sound transfer ready" badge. Honest about which languages we have research-backed listening patterns for vs. which fall back to defaults. The four uploaded reference languages (Spanish, Vietnamese, Hmong, Cantonese) plus expansion list — we don't claim coverage we don't have.
  • No race or ethnicity questions, ever. The product asks about how you talk, not who you are. AAE is a linguistic category, not a racial one — there are speakers from many backgrounds. Step 3's prompt language is deliberate.
  • "We will teach mainstream-school English." Step 3's amber box names the asymmetry: the product respects the home dialect, AND teaches the school dialect explicitly because tests are written in it. This is the only honest position; pretending tests don't exist is a disservice.
  • Quiet pattern-detection backstop. Step 4's last paragraph promises: if the program notices systematic dialect features the family didn't disclose, it asks gently. Never auto-applies a dialect setting based on inference — that crosses a line.
  • School deployment behavior. When a school administers the product, the dialect onboarding should appear in the parent-facing app, not the school-admin app. Teachers should not be setting dialect for students — only families should.
  • Inspired directly by the uploaded SEL glossary PDF. The cross-language sound-spelling transfer charts (English ↔ Spanish/Vietnamese/Hmong/Cantonese) and the AAE linguistic differences guide are the substantive backing for the listening patterns this flow enables.
