## Hook

Imagine you're the conductor of a train, and each verb is a train car that must match the engine, which is the subject. If the cars don't match, the train can't run smoothly. In writing, if subjects and verbs don't agree, sentences can become confusing. Today, we'll learn how to ensure our 'trains' run smoothly by mastering subject-verb agreement.

## Explanation (1896 chars)

Subject-verb agreement means the subject and verb in a sentence must match in number. A singular subject takes a singular verb, and a plural subject takes a plural verb. For example, "The cat runs" because "cat" is singular. If the subject is "cats," the verb changes to "run": "The cats run." 

When subjects are joined by "and," they take a plural verb: "The dog and the cat are friends." With "or" or "nor," the verb agrees with the closest subject: "Neither the teacher nor the students are ready." 

Indefinite pronouns like "everyone" are singular: "Everyone is here." Collective nouns like "team" can be singular or plural: "The team is winning" (as a unit) vs. "The team are arguing" (as individuals). Understanding these rules helps ensure clarity in writing, making communication more effective. Focus on identifying the main subject and ensuring the verb matches it in number. ## Worked example (979 chars)

Consider the sentence: "The list of items is on the desk." Here, "list" is the subject, and it is singular, so we use "is" as the verb. Even though "items" is plural, it is part of a prepositional phrase and does not affect the verb agreement. 

Now consider: "The players on the team run every morning." In this case, "players" is the subject, and it is plural, so the verb "run" is also plural. The phrase "on the team" is a prepositional phrase and does not affect the subject-verb agreement. 

These examples show how identifying the true subject of a sentence is crucial for ensuring correct subject-verb agreement. Always focus on the main subject rather than words that might come between the subject and the verb. This matters because the correct answer is not just a matching word or phrase. It is the choice that fits the whole question and can be defended with a specific detail. The reasoning should explain both what the evidence says and how it supports the answer.

## Common errors

- Confusing singular and plural forms, especially with collective nouns.
- Misidentifying the subject in complex sentences.
- Incorrectly matching verbs with subjects joined by 'or' or 'nor'.

## Sentence frames

- The group of students ___ (is/are) planning a trip.
- Neither the teacher nor the students ___ (was/were) ready for the test.
- Each of the players ___ (has/have) a unique skill.

## Success criteria

- Identify the main subject of a sentence correctly.
- Match the verb to the subject in number consistently.
- Apply rules for subject-verb agreement with compound subjects and collective nouns.

### Practice item 1 — inline-dropdown

**Question**: Choose the correct verb: The bouquet of flowers ___ (is/are) beautiful.

**Sentence**: The bouquet of flowers ___ ([BLANK]/are) beautiful.

**Options**: ['is', 'are', 'were']
**Correct**: is

**Why right**: The correct answer is 'is' because 'bouquet' is the singular subject, and 'of flowers' is a prepositional phrase.

**Distractor rationales**:
- _are_: 'Are' is incorrect because 'bouquet' is singular.
- _were_: 'Were' is past tense and does not match the present tense of the sentence.

**Coach hint**: Identify the main subject of the sentence.
