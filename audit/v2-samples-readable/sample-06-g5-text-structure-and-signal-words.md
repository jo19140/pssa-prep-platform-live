## Hook

Have you ever tried to follow a recipe or build something with instructions? Imagine if those steps were all mixed up! Understanding text structure is like having a clear set of instructions. It helps us see how information is organized, making it easier to understand and remember what we read.

## Explanation (2000 chars)

Text structure refers to how information is organized in a passage. Recognizing text structure helps readers understand the author's purpose and the main ideas. There are several common types of text structures: 

1. **Sequence/Chronological Order**: This structure presents events in the order they happen. Signal words include "first," "next," "then," and "finally."

2. **Cause and Effect**: This structure explains reasons why something happened or the effects of something. Signal words include "because," "therefore," "as a result," and "due to."

3. **Compare and Contrast**: This structure shows how two or more things are similar and different. Signal words include "similarly," "on the other hand," "both," and "however."

4. **Problem and Solution**: This structure presents a problem and then offers one or more solutions. Signal words include "problem," "solution," "solve," and "issue."

5. **Description**: This structure provides details about a topic. Signal words include "for example," "such as," "including," and "characteristics."

Understanding these structures helps readers anticipate what information will come next and how it relates to what they have already read. For example, if a passage uses sequence, you know to look for steps or events in order. If it uses cause and effect, you can look for reasons and outcomes. ## Worked example (1107 chars)

Let's look at a short passage to identify its structure:

"Every morning, Sarah follows a routine. First, she wakes up and brushes her teeth. Then, she eats breakfast. After that, she packs her school bag. Finally, she heads to the bus stop."

In this passage, the structure is **Sequence/Chronological Order**. The signal words "first," "then," "after that," and "finally" indicate the order of events. This structure helps us understand Sarah's morning routine step by step.

Now, consider this passage:

"The playground was empty because it started raining. As a result, the children went inside to play."

This passage uses a **Cause and Effect** structure. The signal words "because" and "as a result" show the reason (rain) and the effect (children went inside). Recognizing these words helps us understand the relationship between the events. This matters because the correct answer is not just a matching word or phrase. It is the choice that fits the whole question and can be defended with a specific detail. The reasoning should explain both what the evidence says and how it supports the answer.

## Common errors

- Confusing signal words for different structures.
- Missing the main idea due to not recognizing the structure.
- Overlooking the author's purpose because of unclear text organization.

## Sentence frames

- The text structure used in this passage is ____.
- Signal words that indicate this structure are ____.
- This structure helps the reader understand ____.

## Success criteria

- I can identify the text structure used in a passage.
- I can recognize signal words that indicate the text structure.
- I can explain how the text structure helps understand the passage.

**Question**: What is the text structure of the following passage? "The rain poured down heavily. Consequently, the streets were flooded, and traffic came to a halt."

- Sequence/Chronological Order
- Cause and Effect **← CORRECT**
- Compare and Contrast
- Problem and Solution

**Why right**: The passage uses a Cause and Effect structure. The signal words "consequently" and "as a result" indicate the effects of the rain.

**Distractor rationales**:
- _Sequence/Chronological Order_: This choice might seem correct if focusing on the order of events, but the passage emphasizes cause and effect.
- _Compare and Contrast_: This choice is incorrect because the passage does not compare two things.
- _Problem and Solution_: This choice is incorrect because the passage does not present a problem and solution.

**Coach hint**: Look for words that show a reason and what happened because of it.

**Sentence**: The team practiced every day. ____, they won the championship [BLANK].

**Options**: ['First', 'As a result', 'Similarly', 'However']
**Correct**: As a result

**Why right**: The correct signal word is "As a result," which indicates the outcome of the team's practice.

**Distractor rationales**:
- _First_: This option is incorrect because it indicates the beginning of a sequence, not a result.
- _Similarly_: This option is incorrect because it indicates a comparison, not a result.

**Coach hint**: Think about what word shows the result of an action.
