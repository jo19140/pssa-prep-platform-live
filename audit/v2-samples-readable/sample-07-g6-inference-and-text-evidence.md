## Hook

Imagine you're a detective trying to solve a mystery. You have clues scattered all around, but you need to piece them together to understand the full story. In reading, making inferences is just like solving a mystery. You gather evidence from the text to uncover hidden meanings and insights. Today, we'll become reading detectives and learn how to use text evidence to make strong inferences.

## Explanation (1922 chars)

Inference is the process of drawing conclusions based on evidence and reasoning. When you read, authors often imply information rather than stating it directly. To understand these implied meanings, you need to make inferences. For example, if a character in a story is shivering and wearing a coat, you might infer that it is cold outside, even if the text doesn't explicitly say so. 

To make a good inference, follow these steps:

1. **Read Carefully**: Pay attention to details in the text. Authors leave clues in descriptions, dialogue, and actions.

2. **Ask Questions**: As you read, ask yourself questions about the characters, setting, and events. What might be happening beneath the surface?

3. **Look for Evidence**: Identify specific parts of the text that support your inference. This could be a sentence, a phrase, or even a single word.

4. **Connect the Dots**: Use your background knowledge and the evidence from the text to draw a logical conclusion.

5. **Explain Your Thinking**: Be ready to explain how you arrived at your inference using the evidence you found.

By practicing these steps, you'll become more skilled at uncovering deeper meanings in texts, making your reading experience richer and more engaging. Remember, every inference should be backed by evidence from the text to ensure it's valid and reliable. For example, students should name the exact clue, connect it to the question, and explain why it proves the answer. ## Worked example (1005 chars)

Let's look at an example from a fictional story:

"As Mia walked into the room, she noticed the curtains drawn tightly and the faint smell of smoke lingering in the air. Her heart raced as she saw the broken vase on the floor."

**Inference**: Something unexpected or possibly dangerous happened in the room.

**Evidence**: The drawn curtains suggest someone wanted to hide the room's interior. The smell of smoke and the broken vase indicate a disturbance or accident.

**Explanation**: By piecing together these clues, we can infer that an event occurred that someone might want to conceal. This inference is supported by the specific details provided in the text, such as the drawn curtains and the broken vase, which suggest secrecy and chaos. This matters because the correct answer is not just a matching word or phrase. It is the choice that fits the whole question and can be defended with a specific detail. The reasoning should explain both what the evidence says and how it supports the answer.

## Common errors

- Failing to connect evidence to the inference.
- Overlooking key details that support the inference.
- Making inferences without sufficient textual evidence.

## Sentence frames

- I infer that ___ because the text states ___.
- The evidence suggests ___, which means ___.
- Based on ___, I can conclude that ___.

## Success criteria

- Identify key details in the text that support inferences.
- Explain how evidence supports the inference.
- Make logical inferences that align with the text.
