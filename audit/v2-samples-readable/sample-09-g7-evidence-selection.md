## Hook

Imagine you're a detective trying to solve a mystery. You have all the clues in front of you, but you need to pick the right ones to solve the case. In reading, selecting evidence is just like that. You need to find the right pieces of information to support your conclusions. Today, we'll learn how to become evidence detectives!

## Explanation (2023 chars)

Selecting evidence from a text is a crucial skill in understanding and analyzing informational texts. When you read, you gather information that helps you draw conclusions or make inferences. However, not all information is equally important. To effectively support your analysis, you must identify and use the most relevant pieces of evidence. 

Start by understanding the question or claim you need to support. This will guide your search for evidence. As you read, highlight or note down key details that relate directly to the question. Look for facts, statistics, or statements that clearly support the main idea or argument. 

Consider the context of the evidence. Ask yourself: Does this piece of information directly relate to the claim? Is it specific enough to provide strong support? Sometimes, a piece of evidence might seem relevant but lacks the depth needed to fully support your analysis. 

Additionally, be aware of the author's purpose and tone. This can influence how evidence is presented and what is emphasized. Understanding these elements can help you discern which pieces of evidence are most aligned with the author's main points. 

Finally, practice makes perfect. The more you practice selecting evidence, the better you'll become at quickly identifying the most compelling pieces of information. This skill not only helps in reading comprehension but also strengthens your writing and critical thinking abilities. For example, students should name the exact clue, connect it to the question, and explain why it proves the answer. A common mistake is choosing a detail that sounds related but does not actually prove the idea. Strong work uses the standard's language and shows the reasoning step by step. ## Worked example (1048 chars)

Let's look at an example. Suppose you are reading a passage about the benefits of exercise. The question asks you to find evidence supporting the idea that exercise improves mental health. 

As you read, you come across a sentence stating, "Regular physical activity releases endorphins, which are chemicals in the brain that act as natural painkillers and mood elevators." This sentence directly supports the claim about mental health benefits. 

Another sentence might say, "Exercise helps maintain a healthy weight." While this is a benefit of exercise, it doesn't relate to mental health, so it wouldn't be the best evidence for this question. 

By focusing on the specific claim and selecting evidence that directly supports it, you ensure your analysis is accurate and well-supported. This matters because the correct answer is not just a matching word or phrase. It is the choice that fits the whole question and can be defended with a specific detail. The reasoning should explain both what the evidence says and how it supports the answer.

## Common errors

- Choosing evidence that is too general or unrelated to the claim.
- Misinterpreting the author's purpose, leading to incorrect evidence selection.
- Overlooking key details that directly support the main idea.

## Sentence frames

- The evidence that best supports the claim is...
- This piece of evidence is relevant because...
- The author uses [evidence] to show...

## Success criteria

- Identify evidence that directly supports the claim or question.
- Ensure the selected evidence is specific and relevant.
- Consider the context and author's purpose when choosing evidence.
