Why the Wolves Came Back

For seventy years, there were no wolves in Yellowstone National Park. People who lived nearby had been reluctant to share the land with them, so the wolves were hunted until none were left.

But scientists noticed something strange. Without the wolves, the elk had nothing to fear. They lingered by the rivers and ate every young tree before it could grow tall. Beavers, who needed those trees, disappeared too.

In 1995, the park brought wolves back. The elk began to move. Young trees survived. Beavers returned and built dams. The shape of the rivers themselves slowly changed. One animal had been holding the whole place together.

Design notes for review

  • Vocab cycle (introduce → use → revisit): Beck/McKeown's robust-vocab pattern. Words come back across days, not just once. The tomorrow-promise on screen 6 is part of that loop.
  • Tap-to-define on passages: the lowest-friction support that doesn't pull the reader out of the text. Words tapped get auto-added to a personal review list — feeding back into future sessions.
  • Comprehension item targets known weak spot: the inference question on screen 5 is explicitly chosen because Maya's profile shows weak cause-effect-across-paragraphs. The session is driven by her gaps, not a generic playlist.
  • Streak + tiny celebrations: light gamification only. We don't want a kid grinding for badges; we want them coming back daily.
  • PSSA hook: the same engine can pull from PSSA-aligned passages when a student is in the PA module — practice screen doesn't need to change.
