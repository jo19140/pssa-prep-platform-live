Design notes · voice conversation during practice

  • The kid can interrupt the program at any point. Screen 2 is the heart of this mockup — mid-passage, the kid asks "what does reluctant mean?" out loud and gets an answer in context. Reading-coach products that aren't conversational can't do this. It's the difference between "the program helps you read" and "the program IS the teacher."
  • Scaffolding before correction, always. Screen 4 — "try it in two parts" comes first, "here's how it sounds" only after attempts. Mirrors how a good human tutor works. Never say "wrong" mid-attempt.
  • Comprehension answers are LLM-graded for gist. Screen 5 — Maya gave a slightly garbled but correct answer. Old MCQ-only systems mark it wrong; an LLM rubric catches that she understood. This is why voice + AI together unlock comprehension assessment for kids who can't write yet.
  • "That's not what I said" override. Always available. Speech recognition isn't perfect, especially for kids' voices, accents, and dysfluent speech. Kid (or adult nearby) can correct what the buddy heard.
  • Silent logging of misconceptions. The buddy doesn't tell the kid "you confused homes with survival." It logs the pattern and addresses it indirectly in future sessions. Preserves dignity.
  • Chat-bubble transcript is hideable. Adult-facing view by default; can be toggled on or off. For kids who get distracted by their own speech transcribed, hide it.
  • "Same time tomorrow?" Closes the session with a habit-building question, not a metric. This is how the program builds the streak naturally — by asking, not by displaying.
  • Dialect-aware decoding evaluation (behavior note). When a family has opted in via dialect onboarding, expected phonological transfers do NOT count as decoding errors. A kid pronouncing "tooth" as "toof" while speaking AAE, or "sheep" as "cheep" with Spanish phonology, is decoding correctly within their home dialect. Engine logs the systematic pattern but doesn't deduct. See [dialect-onboarding.html].
