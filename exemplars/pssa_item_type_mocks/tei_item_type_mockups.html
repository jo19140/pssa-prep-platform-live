After a week, the sunny tray had tall green sprouts. The closet tray had only pale, thin stems. The class decided the plants needed light to grow strong.

Question 11Short Answer · 3 pts

The Puddle Map

By noon, most puddles had dried, but the one near the north wall was still there. The students noticed the north wall blocked the afternoon sun. They decided the shade kept that puddle cool and wet. The class measured each puddle and marked it on a map before they decided.

Write your answer in the box. Use details from the passage.

Why did the puddle near the north wall last the longest? Use two details from the passage to support your answer.

Eligible ContentE03.B-K.1.1.2 (main idea / key details, informational)
Expected answerThe puddle lasted because the north wall blocked the afternoon sun, so the shade kept it cool and wet; the class figured this out by measuring and mapping the puddles before deciding.
Acceptable supportany two: north wall blocked the afternoon sun · the shade kept it cool and wet · they measured each puddle and marked it on a map · most others had dried by noon
PtsWhat earns it
3Correct answer (shade from the wall blocked the sun) plus two specific, accurate text details.
2Partial — some awareness + at least one text detail; may have minor inaccuracy.
1Incomplete/misreads task or no text details — or the answer is entirely copied text.
0Insufficient to score, or inaccurate throughout.
Toy 3-pt response: "It lasted because the north wall blocked the afternoon sun, so the shade kept it cool and wet. The class knew because they measured each puddle and marked it on a map." (answer + two details)
Toy 2-pt response: "Because it was in the shade by the wall." (one idea, no second supporting detail)
Toy 1-pt response: "The one near the north wall was still there." (entirely copied from the passage → capped at 1)
Gates3/2/1/0 rubric present · prompt requires text support · expected answer + acceptable support listed · copied-text cap encoded · score-band examples render

Question 12TDA · 4-pt analytic ×4

The Town Clock That Everyone Trusts

For a hundred years, the brick clock tower has stood in the middle of Maple Town. Shopkeepers set their watches by it. Bus drivers slow down when its bell rings. When a storm once stopped the clock, the whole town felt out of step until it was fixed. People say the clock does not just tell time — it keeps the town together.

Write an essay analyzing how the author develops the idea that the town clock is more than a machine. Use specific evidence from the passage to support your analysis.

Writer's Checklist — PLAN: read the question and the whole passage; organize your ideas. FOCUS: analyze the passage as you write; use evidence; use precise language, varied sentences, transitions; organize with an introduction, body, and conclusion. PROOFREAD: stay focused; use evidence; fix capitalization, spelling, sentence formation, punctuation, word choice.
Eligible ContentE05.E.1.1.2 (analysis across the text; Reporting Category E)
Weight4-point analytic score, weighted ×4 = up to 16 points on the form
Expected claimThe author builds the "more than a machine" idea by showing the clock guides daily life (watches, buses) and that the town feels lost without it — implying it holds the community together.
Acceptable evidenceshopkeepers set their watches by it; bus drivers slow at the bell; the town "felt out of step" when it stopped; "it keeps the town together."
PtsAnalytic behavior (nine dimensions, summarized)
4In-depth analysis of how the author develops the idea; thorough explicit + implicit reasoning; substantial accurate text evidence; strong structure; skillful transitions/vocabulary; few/no errors.
3Sufficient analysis; clear structure; sufficient accurate evidence; appropriate transitions; some errors that seldom interfere.
2Partial/inconsistent analysis; weak structure; vague evidence; errors may interfere with meaning.
1Minimal/inadequate; little or no analysis or text evidence; many errors often interfere.
Toy high-band outline (3–4): intro names the "more than a machine" idea → body shows how the author uses daily-life details (watches, buses slowing at the bell) and the storm that left the town "out of step" to build the clock's role → explains why "keeps the town together" caps the idea → conclusion ties the details to community.
Toy low-band outline (1–2): retells what the clock does (it is old, it rings a bell) without analyzing how the author builds the "more than a machine" idea; little or no evidence. (summary/retell, not analysis)
Gatesall nine rubric dimensions present at all four levels · prompt is genuinely analytic (how/why), not summary/opinion · text-evidence requirement · ×4 weight recorded · Writer's-Checklist preview present

Calibration appendix — Short Answer & TDA rubric behavior

Summarizes how the official PDE rubrics behave so authored items and gates calibrate to real scoring — without copying any released anchor responses. Annotated anchor papers are used later, only to spot-check behavior, after the contract is fixed (so we don't overfit the schema to one paper).

Short Answer (Grade 3) — 3 / 2 / 1 / 0

ScoreBehavior that defines the boundary
3Correct answer and text-based support; specific, accurate details (name/describe/explain/compare).
2Partial: some task awareness + at least one text detail; minor inaccuracy tolerated.
1Incomplete or misreads the task, or no text details — and a response that is entirely copied text cannot exceed 1.
0Insufficient to score or inaccurate throughout.

Authoring/gate implications: the prompt must require both an answer and support (a one-word recall can't reach 3); the reviewer key must list the expected answer core + the set of acceptable supporting details; the gate must encode the copied-text cap so a verbatim lift is flagged, not credited.

Text-Dependent Analysis (Grades 4–8) — 4-point analytic, weighted ×4

DimensionWhat moves a score up
Task / analytic understandingaddresses all parts with in-depth (not surface) analysis of the text
Structureclear introduction → development → conclusion; organization supports the focus
Analysisexplains explicit and implicit meaning, not just retelling
Text evidencesubstantial, accurate, direct reference (details, examples, quotes, facts, definitions)
Languageskillful transitions; precise, domain-specific vocabulary drawn from the text
Conventionsfew/no errors; errors do not interfere with meaning

Authoring/gate implications: the prompt must be analytic ("how/why the author develops X"), never summary or opinion; the reviewer key must carry an expected claim + acceptable evidence + the per-dimension rubric; scoring metadata records the ×4 weight; a summary-only or opinion-only response should land in the low band by rule.
