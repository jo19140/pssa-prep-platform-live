## How a Letter Travels

Have you ever wondered what happens to a letter after you drop it in a mailbox? A single letter takes a long journey before it reaches the right home, and many workers and machines help it along the way.

First, you write your letter, fold it into an envelope, and add a stamp. The stamp is like a ticket that pays for the trip. Then you drop the letter into a blue mailbox on the street, or you leave it in your own mailbox and raise the flag. A raised flag can show that outgoing mail is waiting. Writing the address neatly helps every machine and worker send the letter to the right place.

Soon a mail carrier comes by and collects the waiting letters. The carrier brings them to a building called a sorting center. A sorting center is a large place where mail from many neighborhoods comes together at once. Inside, tall machines read the address and the ZIP code printed on each envelope. A ZIP code is a set of numbers that stands for a certain area of the country. The machines can sort thousands of letters in an hour — far faster than people could ever sort them by hand. A barcode may be printed near the bottom of the envelope. Sorting machines read the barcode to help send the letter toward the correct area. Workers watch over the machines and pull out any letter that is bent, torn, or hard to read, so every piece keeps moving toward its home.

After they are sorted, the letters travel toward the city or town written on the address. Letters going a short distance ride in trucks. Letters headed far away may even fly across the country on an airplane. During this part of the trip, mail may move by road or air across the postal network before continuing toward its final destination. The letters ride in sturdy trays and bins that keep them standing upright and in the right order. At every step, each letter is grouped with other letters going to the same place. Grouping letters by destination helps keep the mail organized.

When the letters reach the local post office, carriers sort them one more time — now by street and by house number. Finally, a carrier loads the mail and follows a route through the neighborhood, slipping each letter into the correct box.

So the next time a letter lands in your mailbox, remember the trip it took. It was collected, read by machines, carried across town, and delivered to your door, all because of a small stamp and a careful plan.

## A Mail Carrier's Day

A mail carrier helps every letter finish its journey. But the job takes much more than walking down the street and dropping off mail. A good carrier uses special tools and follows a careful plan, rain or shine.

The day begins early at the post office. Before heading out, the carrier sorts the mail into the exact order of the route. A route is the planned path a carrier follows through a neighborhood. By putting the mail in order first, the carrier never has to stop and dig around for the next house's letters. Putting the mail in route order takes time, but it saves time later on the street. Over time, a carrier learns the neighborhood well and remembers which homes are easy to miss.

On walking routes, a carrier may use a strong shoulder bag called a satchel. It holds letters, magazines, and small packages. On some curbside routes, carriers use postal vehicles designed to make roadside mailboxes easier to reach.

Carriers also carry a small scanner. When a package must be tracked or needs a signature, the carrier scans it. The scan records information about the package and updates its tracking record, so the family waiting for it can follow its trip. Some packages are too big for the satchel and ride in the truck until the carrier reaches the right address, where the carrier checks the label twice to be sure it goes to the correct home.

Mail carriers work in every kind of weather. They wear sturdy shoes for the long walks and bring water on hot days. They watch carefully for dogs, icy steps, and busy streets. In winter they knock the snow from their boots; in summer they wear hats to block the sun. A friendly wave from a neighbor, or a thank-you note tucked in a mailbox, can make a long day feel lighter. The work is not easy, yet carriers take real pride in bringing the mail to the right home, day after day.

Because of a carrier's patient work, a letter that began as one small envelope finally reaches the very person who has been waiting to read it.
